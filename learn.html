Notation

Six letters are enough to start.

Algorithms are just short turn sequences. Learn the face letters first, then read one move at a time.

R right L left U up D down F front B back

A plain letter turns clockwise. A prime mark turns counterclockwise, like R'. A 2 means turn twice, like U2.

Beginner path

Solve in five passes.

01

White cross

Place the four white edge pieces around the white center. Match each edge's side color to its center.

02

White corners

Finish the first layer by putting each white corner between the three centers that share its colors.

03

Middle edges

Insert the four top-layer edges that do not contain yellow into the middle layer.

04

Yellow face

Make the yellow cross, then turn the yellow corners until the whole top face is yellow.

05

Final layer

Move the yellow corners and edges into their correct positions. When the side colors line up, the cube is solved.

A little context

It began as a teaching object.

Erno Rubik built the cube in 1974 to explore movement in three dimensions. Speedcubing came later: people shared methods, competitions formed, and solving became a practice of pattern recognition and calm repetition.

Practice rhythm

Solve slowly first. Correct turns build recognition faster than rushing through mistakes. Once the method feels familiar, open the timer.
